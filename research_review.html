Following Trusted Accounts, Validating Their Claims

How AI/agentic research pipelines and "follow-the-smart-money" strategies are actually built — and what that implies for a disciplined macro & thematic-equities system.

Prepared for Neo Date 30 June 2026 Scope AI/agentic LLM pipelines · FinTwit copy-trading & signal services Asset context Macro & thematic equities Status Research, not investment advice

You want to follow a handful of vetted writers on X, Bluesky and Substack, pull their theses apart, check the claims against reality, and only act on what survives — trusting a source only as far as its track record earns. That instinct is sound, and it is also where almost everyone goes wrong. This review maps how comparable systems are built, where their edge actually comes from, where it leaks away, and how to turn "I trust this account" into something you can measure and size.

01 — Synthesis

Executive summary

Across both families I reviewed — agentic LLM research systems and social/copy-trading strategies — the same shape recurs. The idea is cheap and abundant; the edge lives in three places that are tedious and therefore neglected: honestly measuring whose calls are worth borrowing, mechanically verifying claims before acting, and holding the position over the horizon where the narrative actually pays. Nine takeaways:

  1. Signal from social/narrative sources is real but conditional and short-lived. LLM-scored news sentiment predicts next-day returns (Lopez-Lira & Tang report ~90% directional hit rates on the initial reaction), and StockTwits sentiment predicts returns mainly around volume spikes and events — but the edge decays fast and shrinks as adoption rises.19
  2. Most "finfluencers" are negatively skilled. The majority of social posters are anti-skilled: their bullishness predicts retail order flow that earns negative returns. A minority are genuinely skilled. Curation is the whole game, and it must be evidence-based, not vibes.8
  3. Copy-trading, the purest "follow the account" product, mostly loses for followers. Provider-side incentives, herding, and the disposition effect work against you; eToro itself discloses 51% of retail CFD accounts lose money. "Mirror a top trader" is the cautionary tale your design must beat, not the template to copy.7
  4. The defensible architecture separates two jobs. LLMs are strong and safe at extraction, structuring and verification of what a source claimed; they are weak and dangerous at prediction. Keep them on the first job.
  5. Verification is a solved-enough engineering problem. Decompose prose into atomic claims, retrieve evidence per claim, and check support — the SAFE / Chain-of-Verification pattern. SAFE matches human fact-checkers 72% of the time at ~1/20th the cost; CoVe materially cuts hallucination by forcing prose into testable questions.23
  6. Track record must be a computed object, not an adjective. TipRanks' three-factor model — hit rate, average return per call, and statistical significance from volume — is a usable template for scoring the accounts you follow, provided you fix timestamping, survivorship and hindsight-edit problems.10
  7. The buy-side adds the layer FinTwit lacks: what's already priced in. A correct thesis on an over-owned name loses money. Mauboussin's reverse-DCF / expectations framing, base rates, and a Klein pre-mortem are the discipline that converts a borrowed idea into a trade with asymmetry.1112
  8. Backtests of LLM judgment are largely contaminated. Models trained on the internet have already seen your test window; "Profit Mirage" shows FinMem leaves 82% of predictions unchanged under perturbation (memorisation, not foresight), and agents that shine on static benchmarks underperform live. Never backtest the LLM's own opinion over its training period.5
  9. Your prototype already has the right spine but the wrong center of gravity. thesis-sidecar's Gate 0–5 and adversarial-PM judge are genuinely good ideas; the mess is schema sprawl (a new JSON object bolted on every eval iteration) and an eval that rewarded presence of sections over decision usefulness. It is missing the one loop that would make it useful: validated thesis → sized position → realised outcome → source re-score.
  10. Your real edge is process and horizon, not idea-generation. This matches your own backtests: the narrative filter helps at ~10-day holds, not at 1 day, and sizing is the lever. Borrowing ideas is fine; the system's job is to be the disciplined, patient, accountable operator the original poster usually is not.
The one-line thesis

Don't build an idea generator — build an accountability engine: a small set of measured sources, a mechanical claim-verifier, a "what's priced in" gate, and a feedback loop that re-scores each source on realised outcomes. The alpha is in the discipline you add after the tweet, and in the patience to hold past day one.

02 — Framing

Scope & the central problem

This review covers the two families you selected. The first is AI/agentic LLM pipelines — the engineering pattern behind systems that read documents, extract assertions and check them. The second is FinTwit copy-trading and signal services — the live, money-on-the-line version of "follow trusted accounts," and the richest source of evidence on what goes wrong. I read both through the lens of macro & thematic equities, which is where your watchlist already sits (Citrini, Lyn Alden, Doomberg, SemiAnalysis, Kuppy and similar).

The problem all of these systems are really solving is one your own prototype README states crisply: social research is "a firehose of confident theses with no accountability," and the failure mode is "chasing a correct idea at the wrong price." Everything below is about installing the accountability and the price-discipline that the source did not.

Three structural facts frame the rest of the document:

  • The idea is not the scarce input. Good macro/thematic theses are published openly and constantly. If your system's value proposition is "we surface ideas," you have no moat.
  • Narrative edges are horizon-bounded. Narratives move prices and then mean-revert toward fundamentals (Shiller; Tetlock). Act too early and you're early; act on the spike and you buy the reversion; hold the right window and you capture the drift.1314
  • Trust is a measurement, not a feeling. "Good track record" has to become a number with a confidence interval, or it will quietly become "accounts I like."
03 — The build pattern

How AI / agentic pipelines work

Strip the branding from AlphaSense, Brightwave, Rogo, Fintool and the academic trading agents and you find one canonical pipeline. Each stage has a well-understood technique and a characteristic failure mode. This is the skeleton your system should be built on.

The stages, concretely

Ingestion & segmentation. Pull the source's long-form artefact (Substack post, X thread, podcast transcript, fund letter), normalise it, and split into retrievable chunks with precise timestamps. Timestamp precision is not cosmetic: it is your only defence against lookahead bias later.

Claim extraction & typing. A thesis is, as your own Gate 1 puts it, "a bundle of claims wearing a trench coat." The model rewrites the prose into discrete assertions and tags each by type — factual, valuation, catalyst, price/technical, or expression — so each can be checked by the right method. Keeping the author's exact quote alongside the paraphrase is important; drift between them is itself a finding.

Atomic decomposition. The verification literature's key insight is that you must check at the granularity of individual facts, not sentences — one sentence often carries several checkable facts (this is exactly what SAFE does before fact-checking).2 "Backlog is growing" and "backlog went $25B→$42B YoY" are different claims with different evidentiary burdens.

Evidence retrieval & verification. For each atomic claim, issue targeted searches or RAG queries against primary sources (filings, regulators, data series), then judge whether the evidence supports, contradicts, or is insufficient — covered in detail in §04.

Synthesis with mandatory citation. The model assembles a verdict where every load-bearing number is bound to a retrieved source. Enterprise tools enforce this — AlphaSense and Fintool both answer with citations to every source used, precisely because an un-cited financial number is a liability.4

Agentic patterns worth borrowing

The generator–critic split (one model drafts, a second adversarially scores) is the pattern behind your own "adversarial PM judge," and behind Reflexion-style self-correction. It works — with a caveat: LLM-as-judge has known biases (verbosity, position, self-preference). Treat the judge as a useful filter, not ground truth, and keep its rubric anchored to externally checkable facts rather than style. Tool use (retrieval, a real DCF calculator, a charting/price API) matters more than cleverer prompting: it moves load-bearing numbers out of the model's imagination and into deterministic code.

04 — The verifier

Claim verification methods

This is the most transferable part of the AI literature for you, because claim-checking — not prediction — is where your system earns its keep. Four methods, in rough order of how directly they map onto your build:

Automated factuality methods and what they actually do
MethodMechanismResult & relevance to you
SAFE
DeepMind, 2024		 Decompose a long answer into individual facts; for each, an agent issues multi-step Google searches and rates it supported / not-supported against results. Agrees with human annotators 72% of the time; wins 76% of sampled disagreements; ~20× cheaper than humans.2 This is essentially your fact-check gate, made mechanical.
Chain-of-Verification
CoVe, 2023		 4 steps: draft answer → plan verification questions → answer them independently → revise. Separates "writing" from "fact-finding," forcing prose into falsifiable questions. Cuts hallucination materially (FActScore 63.7→71.4 on a biography task).3 Use it to interrogate the author's claims, not just your own output.
FActScore
metric, 2023		 Precision metric: fraction of a generation's atomic facts that are supported by a trusted knowledge source. A scoring discipline — gives you a per-report "% of claims verified," far more honest than a pass/fail.
NLI / FEVER entailment
claim ↔ evidence		 Classify each (claim, evidence) pair as supports / refutes / not-enough-info using a natural-language-inference model. The cheap, deterministic core of the "support" judgment; pairs well with retrieval and avoids asking the generator to grade itself.

The common thread — and the design principle to lift wholesale — is decompose, retrieve per claim, judge support, then abstain when evidence is insufficient. Crucially, none of these eliminate error: CoVe's own authors note hallucinations persist for subtle reasoning, opinion and attribution claims.3 So verification grades confidence, it does not certify truth — which is why a human still owns the trade.

Why financial numbers are the danger zone

LLMs hallucinate specific figures with fluent confidence — the single worst failure for a trading system, because a fabricated backlog or margin looks exactly like a real one. Two controls are non-negotiable: (1) every load-bearing number must carry a retrieved citation or it is treated as unverified; (2) computations (multiples, DCF, R:R) run in code on cited inputs, never "in the model's head." Your prototype's instinct here — "never present an uncited factual claim as verified" — is exactly right and should be enforced by the renderer, not by good intentions.

What the academic trading agents tell us — mostly a warning

The research-agent literature (TradingAgents' multi-agent debate, FinMem's hierarchical memory, FinAgent's multimodal memorisation) reports glittering backtests.5 Treat those numbers with deep suspicion. The same literature has now produced its own debunking:

  • Information leakage / lookahead bias is pervasive. An LLM trained through 2024 has already ingested the outcomes for any pre-2024 backtest window — it can retrieve the answer rather than predict it. "Profit Mirage" finds FinMem leaves 82% of its predictions unchanged when inputs are perturbed, the signature of memorisation.5
  • Static-benchmark skill doesn't survive contact with live markets. When evaluated in real time (LiveTradeBench), agents that topped static leaderboards underperformed.5
  • Even sentiment-forecasting alpha decays as everyone adopts it. Lopez-Lira & Tang find strategy returns fall as LLM adoption rises — the edge is real but competed away.1

This is the empirical case for the architectural split in §03: let the LLM read and verify, and refuse to let it (or its backtest) predict over its own training window.

06 — The cautionary family

Follow-the-account, in practice

Copy-trading is "follow trusted accounts" taken to its literal extreme: capital auto-replicates a chosen operator. Studying it tells you exactly which design choices destroy followers — so you can invert them.

How the platforms work

On eToro's CopyTrader, "Popular Investors" expose public portfolios with a risk score, performance history and every live position. A follower allocates a minimum (from $200), and from that point every trade is replicated proportionally, with no extra fee beyond spreads. ZuluTrade and others rank "signal providers" on similar surfaced metrics.7 The ranking metrics are the tell: they emphasise headline return and popularity, which are exactly the metrics most distorted by survivorship and risk-seeking.

The performance verdict

The evidence is sobering and consistent:

  • Most followers lose. eToro's own regulatory disclosure: 51% of retail CFD accounts lose money. General day-trading studies put consistent winners at 10–15%.7
  • Incentives are misaligned. Providers are rewarded for copiers and visibility, which encourages risk-seeking and over-trading; followers inherit the disposition effect and herding without the provider's context or exit timing.
  • Most posters are anti-skilled. The "Finfluencers" study finds the majority are negatively skilled — their bullish sentiment predicts retail order flow that earns negative returns — while a skilled minority genuinely predicts positive returns. Naïvely following the loud average is a wealth-transfer mechanism.8

Signal services & sentiment data

One tier up from copy-trading sit alt-data sentiment vendors (RavenPack, Social Market Analytics, StockTwits feeds) that sell structured sentiment rather than trades. The academic read on raw social sentiment is that it works conditionally: StockTwits-classified sentiment predicts returns around events and message-volume spikes, but not unconditionally day-to-day; Twitter sentiment creates a multi-day mispricing that slowly corrects.9 Tetlock's classic result is the template for the mechanism: high media pessimism pushes prices down then reverts to fundamentals — sentiment is a transient force, not a fundamental one.14

Invert the copy-trading failures into design rules

① Rank sources on risk-adjusted, benchmark-relative, horizon-matched performance — not headline return or follower count. ② Never auto-replicate; borrow the idea, run it through your own gates. ③ Weight by measured skill, and treat unmeasured/anti-skilled sources as zero or contrarian. ④ Assume the edge is transient and size/hold accordingly rather than marrying the position.

07 — The crux

Turning trust into measurement

Your premise — "these accounts must have good track records so we can trust their outputs" — is the right premise and the hardest part to do honestly. The reference template is TipRanks' three-factor analyst model; the hard part is the adjustments it doesn't fully solve.

TipRanks-style scoring, and what it leaves you to fix
FactorWhat it measuresThe trap you must close
Success rate (hit ratio)Share of calls that made money — binary per call, so one huge win can't mask ten losses.10Hit rate ignores magnitude; a 40%-hit source with huge winners can beat a 70%-hit source. Pair it with average return.
Average return per callMean performance across calls — captures overall skill, not lucky single hits.10Absolute return rewards beta and bull markets. Use benchmark-relative (vs sector/SPX) and ideally risk-adjusted return.
Statistical significance (volume)Rankings require a minimum call count (~10–15) so a couple of lucky calls can't top the table.10Low-frequency macro writers may never reach significance; hold their scores as wide confidence intervals, not point estimates.

The four problems no vendor solves for you

  • Timestamp & attribution. You must log the call at the moment it was public, with the exact instrument and direction. Posts get edited; screenshots lie. Capture the call into your own immutable store on the day, or you are scoring a curated highlight reel.
  • Horizon. A call is only right or wrong relative to a holding period. Macro/thematic theses need weeks-to-months windows; grading them at one day (as your own backtests show the live book effectively did) throws away the edge. Fix the horizon per source/claim type before scoring.
  • Survivorship & selection. You notice an account because it was recently right. Counter it by (a) scoring only calls made after you started tracking, and (b) recording the misses and the silent thesis-creep, not just the wins.
  • Base rates. Score each source against the base rate of its claim type — being bullish equities in a bull market is not skill. Mauboussin's point applies directly: anchor on the reference class, then adjust.11
A usable source score

Maintain, per source and per claim-type, a rolling vector: { benchmark-relative hit rate, avg excess return at the matched horizon, n, recency-weighted decay, fact-check pass rate }. Collapse to a single conviction weight ∈ [0,1] with a confidence interval driven by n. This weight feeds position sizing in §09 — a high-skill, well-evidenced source on a verified claim sizes up; an unproven source sizes to a starter or to zero. This is the dashed feedback loop in the diagram, and it is the single highest-value thing to build.

08 — The missing layer

The buy-side validation layer

FinTwit gives you a thesis. The buy-side adds the four checks that decide whether a true thesis is also a profitable one. Your prototype's Gates 3–5 already reach for these; the literature confirms they're the right four.

  • What's already priced in (variant perception). The most important question and the one tweets skip. Mauboussin's expectations investing inverts the DCF: start from price, solve for the growth/margin/return the market already requires, and ask what you believe that differs.11 A correct, consensus thesis with no expectations gap is not a trade.
  • Base rates. Replace the inside-view story with the outside-view frequency: how often do companies/themes like this actually deliver what the thesis assumes?11
  • Pre-mortem & falsification. Klein's pre-mortem — assume the trade has failed, then explain why — surfaces failure modes standard risk analysis misses; "prospective hindsight" has been found to lift the ability to identify reasons for outcomes by ~30%.12 Pre-register the falsifiers before the position exists, exactly as your Gate 5 demands.
  • Position sizing & horizon. The expression and size are where the edge is realised or surrendered. This is consistent with your own research memory: the narrative filter helps at ~10-day holds, not at 1 day, and sizing/vol-targeting is the real lever.
Narrative is fuel, not foundation

Shiller's Narrative Economics argues narratives spread like contagions and genuinely move prices; Tetlock shows sentiment moves prices then reverts to fundamentals.1314 The operational consequence: a narrative edge has a half-life. Your gates should estimate where in the narrative's life a thesis is (early / crowding / exhausted) and refuse the late, crowded, extended setups — which is what your Gate 4 extension check is for.

10 — Honest appraisal

Critique of thesis-sidecar

You asked me to treat the prototype as inspiration but find it messy and its outputs thin. I agree on both counts, and the reasons are specific and fixable. First, credit where due.

What is genuinely good — keep it

  • The Gate 0–5 spine maps almost exactly onto the validated workflow above (source credibility → claim extraction → fact-check → valuation → technical → falsification). The bones are right.
  • The adversarial-PM judge is the generator–critic pattern the AI literature endorses, and the eval changelog shows it working — mean report quality climbed 63 → 88 across iterations.
  • Primary-source discipline ("never present an uncited factual claim as verified") is exactly the right instinct, and rare.
  • Pre-registered falsifiers (Gate 5) match the pre-mortem evidence.

Why it feels messy and the outputs feel thin — diagnosis

  • Schema sprawl. The changelog shows a new first-class JSON object added almost every iteration — valuation_bridge, model_refresh, metric_snapshot, risk_premortem, catalyst_timeline, industry_structure, evidence_audit, variant_view. Each was a reasonable patch, but the sum is a sprawling payload that's hard to hold in your head. That is the messiness.
  • The eval optimised "section presence," not decision usefulness. Because the judge rewarded the existence of rendered sections, the loop's path of least resistance was to add more sections. The judge even warns about "length padding," yet the incentive gradient pushed toward longer, more templated reports — which is precisely why the outputs feel thin despite being long. Verbosity went up; decisions-per-page did not.
  • One thesis per run, no memory. Each run is a standalone artefact. There is no portfolio view, no source-level track record accumulating across runs, and — critically — no link from a validated thesis to a sized position to a realised outcome. The system can't learn which sources actually pay.
  • It validates ideas but never closes the loop. The whole premise is "trusted accounts," yet nothing measures whether a given account's validated theses subsequently made money. Trust stays an input assumption instead of becoming an earned, updated score.
  • Backtest gap. Your memory notes "no backtest yet." Given the leakage findings (§05), that's almost a feature — but it means conviction is currently un-calibrated. The fix is outcome-logging forward (stage 9), not LLM-judgment backtesting backward.
The core inversion

thesis-sidecar is built as a report generator that happens to validate. It should be built as a decision-and-accountability system that happens to emit a report. The report is an artefact of the process, not the product. Re-centre on the decision (size or don't, hold how long) and the loop (did it work, re-score the source), and the schema can shrink dramatically.

11 — What to build

Design recommendations

Seven concrete moves for the new system, ordered by leverage.

R1 Build the source track-record loop first

Before any new gates, build stage 9 + stage 1: an immutable log that captures each source's dated calls, scores realised benchmark-relative return at a matched horizon, and emits a per-source conviction weight with a confidence interval. This is the thing that operationalises "trusted accounts" and the thing the prototype most lacks.

Keep from sidecar: the tiered watchlist config — but make tier a computed output, not a hand-set input.

R2 Collapse the schema to five first-class objects

Replace the ~8 bolted-on payload objects with: source (+ live score), claims[] (typed, with verification verdict + citation), priced_in (expectations gap), decision (size, horizon, kill-switches), outcome (filled later). Everything currently scattered across objects is a field on one of these five.

Keep: claim typing and the citation/evidence discipline — just attach them to claims[].

R3 Make verification mechanical with the SAFE/CoVe pattern

Decompose to atomic claims, retrieve per claim, judge support via entailment, and emit a FActScore-style "% of load-bearing claims verified." Surface that number at the top of the report. A thesis below a verification threshold never reaches sizing.

Keep: Gate 1 (extraction) and Gate 2 (fact-check) — this is their mechanised form.

R4 Fix the eval so it rewards decisions, not sections

Re-weight the judge toward decision-usefulness and verified-claim density, and add a hard penalty for length without added decision content (the judge already names this; make it bite). Add a realised-outcome term once stage 9 produces data — the only ungameable score.

Keep: the adversarial-PM judge and negative-control thesis — excellent ideas, just re-pointed.

R5 Make the output decision-first

One screen at the top: verdict (size / starter / wait / pass), conviction weight, % claims verified, the expectations gap, the horizon, and the three kill-switches. Everything else is appendix. This directly answers "outputs not very useful" — a PM should decide in 60 seconds, which is your judge's own bar.

R6 Enforce the LLM/prediction firewall

Codify that LLMs extract, verify and structure; they do not forecast returns, and the system never backtests LLM judgment over the model's training window. Timestamp everything at ingestion to keep retrieval honest. This is your defence against the leakage that invalidates most LLM-trading results.

R7 Size by conviction and hold to horizon

Position size = vol-target × conviction weight (source × verification × expectations gap × narrative-stage). Pre-commit the holding horizon (~10 days+ for thematic) at entry. This is where your documented edge lives, and where copy-trading followers and the original posters both fail.

13 — Decisions ahead

What to decide next

To move from review to build, four choices are yours to make:

  • Source set & cadence. Which 5–8 accounts make the v1 vetted set, and how often do you ingest? (I'd start narrow and let the track-record loop earn additions.)
  • Horizon policy. One holding horizon, or per-claim-type horizons? Your 10-day finding argues for a thematic default with catalyst-driven exceptions.
  • Verification depth. Full per-claim retrieval from day one, or a lighter pass that flags the load-bearing 2–3 claims first? (The latter ships faster.)
  • Outcome capture. Paper-trade log vs broker integration for stage 9. Even a manual log beats nothing — it's the loop that compounds.

If useful, the natural next deliverable is a tight spec for the new system built on the five-object schema (R2) and the source-score loop (R1) — small enough to actually build, unlike a tenth payload object.
